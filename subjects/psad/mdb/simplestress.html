Problem 4: Column Post with Wooden Post and Concrete Footing

The column consists of a wooden post and a concrete footing, separated by a steel bearing plate. Find the maximum safe value of the axial load P if the working stresses are 1000psi for wood and 450 psi for concrete.

üé• Watch Tutorial Video

Why do we choose the lesser load even if the question asks the "maximum safe value"? This is a common misconception for learners due to the word "maximum", which makes it tempting to choose the largest value. However, we choose here the least value because it is essentially the maximum load we can apply such that both the wooded and concrete members will not fail.

Think of it like this:

If we apply the load based on concrete, which is 64800lb, then concrete will be able to resist it, but the wooden member would fail because it can only sustain 50265lb. Therefore, 50265lb is the maximum load we can apply so that both the wooden post and concrete footing will not fail by yielding.

Here, we first cut the cable at the lowest point (at the middle since the parabolic cable is symmetrical). Problem 14: Punching Shear Stress with Factor of Safety

What minimum force is required to punch a 20-mm-diameter hole in a plate that is 25mm thick? The shear strength of the plate is 340MN/m2. Factor of safety = 1.5.

üé• Watch Tutorial Video

Shear stress formula:

$\tau = \dfrac{V}{A}$

Given: $\tau = 340 \text{ MPa}$

Substitute the values:

$340~\text{MPa} = \dfrac{V}{\pi d (t)}$

$340~\text{MPa} = \dfrac{V}{\pi (20)(25)}$

Therefore, $V \ge 534.07~\text{kN}$

The answer is 535kN since it is greater than 534.07.

Why do we avoid using the factor of safety in this problem? The question states that we need the force required to "punch" a hole, which means that we want the plate to fail by shearing. Thus, we do not use the factor of safety. We only use the factor of safety if we are interested in ensuring the resistance of bodies or members.
